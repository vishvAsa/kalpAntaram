Accessibility of Vedas in Kāśī during the period between 14th and 17th centuries

Dr. Satyan Sharma,
Independent researcher,
Chandigarh,
April 26, 2026

Intro

Accessibility of Vedic knowledge is a debated issue. A long line of traditional scholars believes that Vedic knowledge directly rooted in Vedas is only for the Brāhmaṇa, Kṣatriya and Vaiśya males who have duly undergone the upanayana saṃskāra (sacred thread ceremony). The dissemination of such knowledge begins with the memorisation of the Veda by hearing the recitation from one’s own Guru. This memorisation of Veda is called vedādhyayana.1

It is well known that Kāśī (aka Varanasi) has been the center of śāstraic studies, and still is. Did the scholars of Kāśī think about such Vedic knowledge in a similar manner or did they have a different practice? I’ll answer this question in two parts.

Nanda Paṇḍita

In the first part I’ll present a statement of Nanda Paṇḍita Dharmādhikārī, a scholar from Kāśī, who belonged to a period between the 16th and 17th centuries CE. In the second part I’ll present some evidence from a period between 14th to 15th century Kāśī which seems to suggest that Paṇḍita’s statement does not represent something new, but is the result of an already existent practice.

Part 1

Nanda Paṇḍita Dharmādhikārī hailed from a scholarly family residing in Kāśī. At the request of king Keśava Nāyaka, he wrote a detailed commentary on the Viṣṇu Smṛti, a text of dharmaśāstra. In his commentary on aphorisms 23 to 25 of the fifth chapter, he quotes an aphorism of Gautama Dharmasūtra, which prohibits a Śūdra from hearing Veda with an intention to memorise it.

The 2 prohibition technically means that hearing Veda with such intention will led a Śūdra to incur pāpa (unseen demerit). Paṇḍita restricts its application to a situation where one hears it directly through arrogance. He says that if one keeps a Brāhmaṇa in between the reciter and listener, no fault is 3 incurred. So, as per him, a Śūdra can hear Vedas in such a setting. Because the context of the aphorism is the hearing of the Vedas for memorisation, it seems that Paṇḍita did not find any fault with the memorisation of Vedic portions which resulted from such hearing.

1

Paṇḍita’s view seems to be based on Vedavyāsa’s statement found in Mahābhārata. A great number 4 of traditional scholars interpret this statement as an injunction of hearing Itihāsas and Purāṇas. The natural context in which the verse occurs is that of teaching and learning Vedas. Paṇḍita seems to 5 have taken the verse in such a context. Was Paṇḍita going against a living tradition, of which he was a part? To answer this, we need to go one or two centuries back.

Kabīra Dāsa

Part 2

Śrī Kabīra Dāsa was a resident of Kāśī in the period between the 14th and 15th centuries CE. His compositions show references to śāstraic terminology, leading to the inference that he had some kind of an access to śāstras.

In one of his compositions he mentions a statement of the Chāndogya Upaniṣad, i.e., ’tat tvam asi’ and tells that it is the message of Upaniṣads. In the same he mentions 6 the conversation between Yājñavalkya and Janaka, which forms a part of the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad. In another of his compositions, he has translated a verse from Muktika Upaniṣad. 7 8

Traditionally Upaniṣads have been seen as a part of Vedas. So whatever rules are applicable to the teaching and learning of Vedas, are applicable to that of Upaniṣads as well. How did Śrī Kabīra Dāsa learn about the contents of Upaniṣads? More importantly how did he recite a statement of the Chāndogya Upaniṣad, which is a veda-vākya from traditional point of view?

Conclusion

2
Conclusion

Now let us move ahead in time and revisit Nanda Paṇḍita’s statement. He says that anyone could hear Veda so long as there is a Brāhmaṇa seated in between the reciter and listener. Basically, the recitation is intended for a Brāhmaṇa, but is being heard by others also. Similar would be the case where a Brāhmaṇa is listening to the meaning and explanation of Vedas, and others are also listening to it.

One may be tempted to think that this was some revolutionary step taken by certain scholars, including Paṇḍita. But the way he puts forth his statement, seems to point to an already active tradition, especially when the contents of Śrī Kabīra Dāsa’s compositions are seen.

Further, the aforementioned indirect hearing is not vedādhyayana per se. Access to Vedas via non-vedādhyayana methods has been known since at least the 7th century CE. Hence, such a scenario was not something which happened in the 14th century for the first time.

Kumārila Bhaṭṭa

Kumārila Bhaṭṭa’s (7th century CE) statement in Tantravārttika (1.3.7) tells us that Śūdras could learn Vedas by methods such as reading a manuscript:

yathaivānyāya-vijñād
vedāl lekhyādi-pūrvakāt |
śūdreṇādhigatād vāpi
dharmajñānaṃ na sammatam ||

Vācaspati Miśra

Vācaspati Miśra (9th century CE) clearly states in his sub-commentary on Brahmasūtra-Śāṅkarabhāṣya (1.3.34) that a Śūdra could understand the Vedas by methods such as reading a manuscript, et cetera, but he won’t receive the unseen merit (puṇya):

pustakādi-paṭhita-svādhyāya-janyo’rthāvabodhaḥ
śūdrāṇāṃ na phalāya kalpata iti

Non adhyayana

Kāśī being the center of śāstraic studies, evidence of such a practice in the period between the 14th and 17th centuries is worth noting. It seems that Vedas, through the non-vedādhyayana methods, were accessible to those beyond the men of Brāhmaṇa, Kṣatriya and Vaiśya varṇas in Kāśī of the said period. If this is the state of Vedas, a wide access to the knowledge of other śāstras logically follows.9
