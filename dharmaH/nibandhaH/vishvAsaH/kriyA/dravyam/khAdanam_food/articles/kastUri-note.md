## bali
Vast majority of devout H are already meat eaters but are well aware of religious norms/ācāra. They consume meat only on certain days, never visit temple on those days or perform religious rituals. I personally know a family of advija friends who ritually offer goats to their village deity with multi-day celebration. The terminal day involves cooking of dal/rice ritually on the temple premises and the bali of the goats. They actually observe various vratas and avoid meat for a specific period leading to the bali day.

With government clamping down on bali in temples, they workaround the issue by taking the aja, getting it washed by the priest and take the aja to an adjacent open ground, slaughter and eat it.

What is understood subconsciously, just needs to be coherently articulated.

## abhojya - arbitrary norms
1\. We have our own concepts of abhakṣya and abhojya, (amedya, apeya etc. are similar concepts). Food that is inherently forbidden and food that is conditionally forbidden. Examples of the latter are hair or insects falling into the food (pollution), milk from an animal for 10 days after it gives birth (compassion). 

Consult dharmaśāstra and social norm to establish non-negotiables, a line that should not be crossed. From the protein argument, we have already established that moral boundaries of different groups are inherently arbitrary. This is what forms the baseline for us - not ahimsā or pseduo-scientific sattvic food theories.

## dvijas and sAdhanA
2\. These rules matter mainly for dvijas who are constantly engaged in their ordained karmans or mantra sadhana.

This group would be a micro-minority and violating the abhojya rules is our internal matter. Our duty to perform rituals entail maintaining a pure ritual space and ritual implements that include one's own body.

In that context avoiding ritually polluting food is a mandatory requirement. This requirement extends to everyone who enters the ritual space divjas or advijas. It is a social contract between various participants to maintain the ritual requirements in the shared space.


3\. The 3 guṇa theory is unscientific but the practitioners know there's a difference in the quality of sadhana or karma when one adheres to all the rules. Instead of arguing the indefensible, we can at least suggest this theory as a modeling tool.

Netflix learns from your watch history and preferences and categorizes you to present targeted ads or recommend suggested content to view next. The guṇa theory could similarly be a modeling tool that could be built based on the patient's dietary practice, exercise habits, gut microbiome profile, place of dwelling etc. 

We can empirically test if this data can indeed model across 3 dimensions and the guṇa is simply the dimensional label here. Not a scientific truth claim. What you eat can affect your gut and your gut health can have neurological effects shouldn't be controversial at all. 

We can even test if adding more dimensions should be added and we have positively contributed to the progression of H thoughts.

### Controlled transgression
4\. IMO even practicing dvijas should optimize for health and maybe break abhojya rules in a controlled manner. Dietary restrictions have evolved into insane levels - avoid this vegetable, avoid that fruit, avoid these grains/pseudo-grains/beans.

Perform trisuparna rite of aghamarshana rite for prāyaścitta. I wouldn't probably go as far as a state requiring kūśmāṇḍa.(I'm being little bit hypocritical and I do largely follow the rules. The price I pay is a large set of supplements which should ideally come from my food.)
