At its most fundamental level, what is ritual impurity?

Whenever a foreign substance is added to a pure substance, such that the latter is now no longer able to fulfill its intended purpose, the former is considered an “impurity”. 

The same substance—which was foreign to the pure substance and caused it to become a mixture—will itself be “pure” in another context for another intended purpose. 

The same logic—albeit ritual/metaphysical not mundane/scientific—will apply to such substances in the ritual context. The same logic will also apply to physical states, particular acts, etc. 

## Rajasvala
Rajasvala as a state does not cohere with the rest of the ritual set-up in most ritual systems. The presence of those in this state is a “foreign substance” that upsets the ritual whole that has been carefully created at the temple (through properly executed rituals over a period of time). 

However, the same state can be a valid ritual input and therefore “pure” in certain non-mainstream ritual systems (the worship of Ucchiṣṭamātaṅgi, etc). 

## Mixutre + purification
Sometimes, a ritual “mixture” needs to be achieved because the added/foreign/“impure” element produces a potent effect in combination with the rest of the regular ritual composition but it nonetheless remains an impurity needing subsequent removal. 

In short, the ritual makes it mandatory for you to do something (for the success of the ritual) that needs later rectification. If you have a simplistic understanding of ritual, this may not have caught your attention.

### Aśvamedha
Examples:

Aśvamedha: Priests and royal wives (and their entourages) will have to utter profanities, which ritually realize the surpassing of the limit of speech and therefore the acquisition of all objects of desire (which can be comprised in speech).

They then utter a remedial verse (which is recited in the daily sandhyāvandana rite) to purify themselves lest the Devas leave them for the lingering taint of impure speech. 

### Rājasūya
Rājasūya: The araṇis are taken by a priest (on the king’s behalf) to the homes of different individuals, some of whom are considered ritually unfit. But this is necessary for the ritual consolidation of the king’s rule over all aspects of his kingdom. Once completed, the priests have to make an offering to Soma-Rudra to drive away the “darkness” caused by the araṇis' contact with the ritually unfit. 

### Mahotsava
Abt 2 millennia later, in the Kāmikāgama’s chapter on Mahotsava:

A crucial aspect of the annual Mahotsava, the processional deity (utsavamūrti) needs to go around the city/town. This is the bestowal of grace and the Kāraṇāgama expressly notes that this procession is for the benefit of Dhūrtas (among others) who merely happen to glance at the deity. 

However, when the deity returns to the temple, the priests have to perform rituals to mitigate the “ritual pollution” caused by the sight of the ritually/spiritually unfit that has fallen on the Deity. 

### Conclusion
The above are three distinct examples—with the same ritual logic. 

However, it is not up to us to determine when we should adhere to a “pure ritual composition” and when we should get things exciting “and mix it up”. 

That’s why we have scriptures. When, why, where and how we should introduce an “impurity” into a ritual system and how we should remove it once the goal is accomplished—all these matters are covered in the Śāstras.
