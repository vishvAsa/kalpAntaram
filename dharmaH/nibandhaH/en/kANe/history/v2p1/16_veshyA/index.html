Vesya

[[P636]] [[Vesya‚ÄĒ(Courtezan)|VeŇõyńĀ‚ÄĒ(Courtesan)]]. This work dealing among other matters with the position of women and marriage in India would be incomplete if nothing were said about prostitutes and concubines. Prostitution has existed from the dawn of history in all countries and in the absence of statistics it is difficult to say whether it flourished more in one country than in another or whether it existed to a greater or lesser extent in ancient days as compared with modern times. The article in the [[Enoyolop√¶dia Britannica|Encyclop√¶dia Britannica]] on prostitution will be an eye-opener to many who will be inclined to turn their nose at Indian conditions.

From the [[Rgveda|ŠĻögveda]] we find that there were women who were common to several [[mon|men]] i.e., who were [[courtezans|courtesans]] or prostitutes [[In Rg.|In ŠĻög.]]. I. 167. 4 the bright [[Maruts|Maruts]] (storm gods) are said to have become associated with the young (lightning), just as men become associated with a young [[courtezan|courtesan]]. 1 It has already been seen how in [[Rg.|ŠĻög.]] II. 29. 1 reference is made to a woman who gives birth in secret to a child and leaves it aside. In [[Rg.|ŠĻög.]] I. 66. 4, I. 117. 18, I. 134. 3 and other places [[jńĀra|jńĀra]] (paramour or secret lover) is spoken of. In [[Gaut.|Gautama]] XXII. 27 it is said that for killing a woman who is a [[brńĀhmaŠĻái|brńĀhmaŠĻáńę]] by birth only and who subsists by harlotry no [[prńĀya citta|prńĀyaŇõcitta]] is necessary but eight [[hand. fule|handful]] of corn may be gifted. Manu IV, 209 forbids [[&|a]] [[brabmana|brńĀhmaŠĻáa]] from taking food offered by harlots (vide also IV. 219); and Manu IX 259 requires the king to punish clever (or deceitful) harlots. In the [[MahabbńĀrata|MahńĀbhńĀrata]] [[courtezans|courtesans]] are an established institution. The [[Adiparva|ńÄdiparva]] (115, 39) narrates how a [[vesy &|veŇõyńĀ]] waited upon [[DhstarńĀŠĻ£tra|DhŠĻõtarńĀŠĻ£ŠĻ≠ra]] when his wife [[Gandhari|GńĀndhńĀrńę]] was pregnant. 2 In the [[Udyogaparva|Udyogaparva]] (30. 38) [[Yudhisthira|YudhiŠĻ£ŠĻ≠hira]] sends greetings to the [[vetyńĀs|veŇõyńĀs]] of the [[Kauravas|Kauravas]]. [[Courtezans|Courtesans]] are describ. ed as going out to welcome [[Krsna|KŠĻõŠĻ£ŠĻáa]] when he came on a mission of peace to the [[Kaurava|Kaurava]] court (Udyoga 86. 15). When the [[Pandava|PńĀŠĻáŠłćava]] armies are described as about to start for battle it

[[P637]] is said that [[oarts|carts]], markets and [[courtezans|courtesans]] also accompanied them (Udyoga 151. 58). Vide also [[Vanaparva|Vanaparva]] 239, 37, [[Karnaparva|KarŠĻáaparva]] 94. 26. [[YńĀj.|YńĀj√Īavalkya]] 3 II. 290 divides concubines into two sorts, [[avaruddha|avaruddhńĀ]] (one who is kept in the house itself and forbidden to have intercourse with any other male) and [[bhujisya|bhujiŠĻ£yńĀ]] (concubine who is not kept in the house, but elsewhere and is in the special keeping of a person) and prescribes a fine of fifty [[paŠĻáas|paŠĻáas]] against another person having intercourse with them. [[NńĀrada|NńĀrada]] 4 ([[stripumsa|strńępuŠĻÉsa]], 78-79) says ‘intercourse is permitted with wanton women ([[svairipi|svairiŠĻáńę]]) who are not [[brahmana|brńĀhmaŠĻáa]] by caste, with a prostitute, a female slave, or a female not restrained by her master, if these belong to a caste lower than oneself; but it is forbidden with women of superior caste. But when these very women are the kept mistresses (of a person) intercourse with them by a stranger is as much a crime as intercourse with another’s wife. Such women must not be approached, though intercourse with them is not forbidden (on the ground of caste &c.), since they belong to another.’ On [[YńĀj.|YńĀj√Īavalkya]] II. 290 the [[Mit.|MitńĀkŠĻ£arńĀ]], after quoting the [[SkandapurńĀŠĻáa|SkandapurńĀŠĻáa]] to the effect that prostitutes constitute a separate caste being sprung from certain [[Apsurases|Apsarases]] (heavenly nymphs) called [[pa√ĪicacŇędńĀs|pa√ĪcacŇęŠłćńĀs]], states that such [[courtezans|courtesans]] as are not kept specially by a person do not [[inour|incur]] any sin, nor punishment at the hands of the king, if they have intercourse with men of the same or [[&|a]] superior caste, nor do men approaching them incur any penalty if they are not [[avaruddha|avaruddhńĀ]]. But the men who visit [[tbem|them]] incur sin (which is an unseen result), since the [[smŠĻõtis|smŠĻõtis]] ordain that men should be devoted to their wives (vide [[YńĀj.|YńĀj√Īavalkya]] I. 81) and the [[prńĀjńĀpatya|prńĀjńĀpatya]] penance is [[presoribed|prescribed]] for him who has intercourse with a [[vesyńĀ|veŇõyńĀ]] (vide [[Atri|Atri]] v. 271). [[NńĀrada|NńĀrada]] 5 ([[vetanasyanapakarma|vetanasyńĀnapakarma]] 18) lays down that if a public woman after getting her fee refuses to receive the customer, she shall pay twice the amount of the fee and the same fine shall be imposed on a man who does not pay the (stipulated) fee, after having had [[interoourse|intercourse]] with a [[veŇõyńĀ|veŇõyńĀ]]. Vide [[YńĀj.|YńĀj√Īavalkya]] II. 292 and [[MatayapurńĀŠĻáa|MatsyapurńĀŠĻáa]] 227. 144-145 for

[[P638]] similar provisions. The [[MatsyapuriŠĻáa|MatsyapurńĀŠĻáa]] chap. 70 dilates upon [[vesyńĀdbarma|veŇõyńĀdharma]]. The [[KńĀmasŇętra|KńĀmasŇętra]] I. 3. 20 defines a [[ga√ĪikńĀ|gaŠĻáikńĀ]] as a [[veŇõyńĀ|veŇõyńĀ]] who is most accomplished and proficient in the 64 [[kalńĀs|kalńĀs]]. [[Apararka|AparńĀrka]] (p. 800, on [[YńĀj.|YńĀj√Īavalkya]] II. 198) quotes [[NńĀrada|NńĀrada]] (cited above) and several verses of the [[MatsyapurńĀŠĻáa|MatsyapurńĀŠĻáa]] about [[vesgńĀs|veŇõyńĀs]].

Concubines being recognized by society, the [[sm√Ĺtis|smŠĻõtis]] provided for their maintenance. During the [[life-time|lifetime]] of a person keeping a concubine, the latter has no legal right to proceed against [[tbe|the]] former. [[NńĀrada|NńĀrada]] ([[dńĀyabbńĀga|dńĀyabhńĀga]] 6 52) and [[Katyayapa|KńĀtyńĀyana]] lay down that even when the property of a deceased person escheats to the king for want of heirs (except in the case of the deceased being a [[brńĀhmana|brńĀhmaŠĻáa]]) the king has first to provide for the [[main. tenance|maintenance]] of the concubines of the deceased, of his slaves and for his [[grńĀddbas|ŇõrńĀddhas]]. The [[Mit.|MitńĀkŠĻ£arńĀ]] says that [[tbe|the]] concubines here referred to are those called [[avaruddhńĀ|avaruddhńĀ]] (and not [[bhujiŠĻ£ya|bhujiŠĻ£yńĀ]]) and that even the kept mistresses of a deceased [[brńĀhmaŠĻáa|brńĀhmaŠĻáa]] are entitled to maintenance from his property. The Privy Council in Bai Nagubai v. Bai Monghibai have gone beyond the [[Mit.|MitńĀkŠĻ£arńĀ]] and have held that all concubines (whether kept in the house practically as members of the family i.e., [[avaruddhńĀ|avaruddhńĀ]] or not so kept i.e., [[bhujigyńĀ|bhujiŠĻ£yńĀ]]) within the exclusive keeping of a Hindu till his death are entitled to maintenance from his property after his death, provided they are continuously chaste thereafter. 7

The rights of the illegitimate children of concubines to inheritance or maintenance will be dealt with later on under inheritance.

Out of the [[samskńĀras|saŠĻÉskńĀras]] enumerated above (pp. 195‚Äď197), the [[MahńĀyaj√Īas|MahńĀyaj√Īas]], [[Utsarjana|Utsarjana]] and [[UpńĀkarma|UpńĀkarma]] will be described under the next chapter, viz., [[ńĀhnika|ńĀhnika]] and [[antyeŠĻ£ti|antyeŠĻ£ŠĻ≠i]] under [[fauce|Ňõauca]].
